Clinical quick reference

What your patients need to know

Product
Anti-na® SIPS — dissolvable dietary supplement stick pack. Lemon-ginger-honey flavor. Dissolves in 3–5 seconds in hot or cold water.
Indication
Adjunct support for chemotherapy-induced nausea and vomiting (CINV, ICD-10: R11). Also used for GLP-1 medication nausea, morning sickness, and motion sickness. Complementary to standard antiemetic protocols per NCCN guidelines — not a replacement.
Active ingredients
Ginger root extract — 2,000mg per serving
Acts as a 5-HT3 antagonist and NK1 modulator. Supports gastric motility. Validated in 50+ peer-reviewed CINV studies at this dose (Ryan et al., 2012; Zick et al., 2009).

Bioenergy Ribose® (D-Ribose) — 1,000mg per serving
Rate-limiting substrate for ATP synthesis via the pentose phosphate pathway. Addresses mitochondrial energy depletion associated with cytotoxic chemotherapy (Teitelbaum et al., 2006).
Delivery
Sublingual / oral dissolution. Bypasses hepatic first-pass metabolism. Onset typically 5–15 minutes. Suitable for patients with mucositis or dysphagia who cannot swallow capsules.
Manufacturing
FDA-registered facility · WADA banned substance tested · GMP-certified · No IND/NDA required (DSHEA-compliant dietary supplement) · Made in Irvine, CA · No added sugar · No artificial additives · No stimulants · No sedatives

Drug interactions

Interaction summary

The following summary reflects published pharmacological data on ginger root extract and D-Ribose at the doses contained in Anti-na® SIPS. This is not an exhaustive list. Always apply clinical judgment for individual patient circumstances.

Drug / Class Status Notes
Carboplatin, Cisplatin, Oxaliplatin
Platinum-based agents		 No known interaction Ginger and D-Ribose have no known pharmacokinetic interactions with platinum agents. Multiple clinical trials have used ginger alongside platinum-based regimens (Ryan et al., 2012).
Paclitaxel, Docetaxel
Taxanes		 No known interaction No documented interactions at 2,000mg ginger dose. Ginger does not significantly inhibit CYP3A4 at this dose range.
5-Fluorouracil (5-FU), Capecitabine
Antimetabolites		 No known interaction No known pharmacokinetic or pharmacodynamic interactions with ginger or D-Ribose.
Ondansetron, Granisetron
5-HT3 antagonists		 Complementary Ginger acts on the same receptor pathway (5-HT3) via natural gingerols. NCCN guidelines support ginger as an adjunct to 5-HT3 antagonists, not a replacement. No adverse interaction documented.
Dexamethasone
Corticosteroid antiemetic		 No known interaction Commonly co-administered with ginger in CINV clinical trials. No interaction documented.
Warfarin, Apixaban, Rivaroxaban
Anticoagulants		 Use with caution Ginger has mild antiplatelet activity and may potentiate anticoagulant effects. Monitor INR closely in patients on warfarin. Discuss benefit/risk with patient. D-Ribose has no anticoagulant interaction.
Insulin, Metformin, GLP-1 agonists
Diabetes medications		 Monitor Ginger may have mild hypoglycemic effects. D-Ribose is metabolized differently from glucose and does not cause typical glycemic spikes, but monitor in insulin-dependent patients. Generally well-tolerated alongside GLP-1 medications (semaglutide, tirzepatide).
Semaglutide, Tirzepatide
GLP-1 receptor agonists		 No known interaction No pharmacokinetic interactions documented. Ginger may complement gastric motility effects. SIPS is increasingly used by GLP-1 patients for medication-induced nausea.
NSAIDs (Ibuprofen, Naproxen) Monitor Additive antiplatelet effect possible. Generally low clinical significance at 2,000mg ginger unless patient is on concurrent anticoagulation.

This interaction summary reflects available published literature and is intended as a clinical reference, not a substitute for individual patient assessment. For the full study library with PubMed citations, see sips.science/studies.

Dosing guidance

Suggested patient protocol

Chemotherapy / CINV

Dosing range
1–4 servings daily
(2,000–8,000mg ginger · 1,000–4,000mg D-Ribose)
Timing
Begin 1–2 hours before infusion. Continue through acute CINV window (24–48 hrs post-treatment).
Method
Dissolve in 6–8oz hot or cold water. Sip slowly — do not chug. Sublingual contact enhances absorption.
Start
Begin with 1 serving and titrate to patient response. No established maximum.

GLP-1 / Morning sickness

Dosing range
1–2 servings daily
as needed for nausea episodes
Timing
Take at onset of nausea, or preventively on injection days for GLP-1 users.
Pregnancy
2,000mg ginger dose has been used in pregnancy nausea research. Advise patient to discuss with OB prior to use.
Method
Dissolve in 6–8oz water. Hot water preparation recommended for morning sickness (soothing effect).
